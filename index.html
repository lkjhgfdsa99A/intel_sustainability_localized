1968: Intel Founded

The Birth of Intel

On July 18, 1968, Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore, two former Fairchild Semiconductor employees, founded Intel Corporation (originally named N M Electronics). The name "Intel" was derived from the words "integrated" and "electronics."

With initial funding of $2.5 million, Intel set up its first headquarters in Mountain View, California, before later moving to Santa Clara.

Key Founders:
  • Robert Noyce - Co-inventor of the integrated circuit
  • Gordon Moore - Known for "Moore's Law" (the observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles approximately every two years)
  • Andy Grove - Joined shortly after founding and later became CEO
1971: First Microprocessor

The 4004 Microprocessor

In November 1971, Intel created history by introducing the 4004, the world's first commercially available microprocessor. This revolutionary 4-bit central processing unit (CPU) consolidated the computer's processing unit onto a single chip.

Originally designed for a Japanese calculator company called Busicom, the 4004 contained 2,300 transistors and could perform about 60,000 operations per second‚Äîa groundbreaking achievement for its time.

Key Features:
  • 4-bit processor
  • Clock speed: 108 KHz
  • 2,300 transistors using 10Œºm technology
  • Instruction cycle time: 10.8 microseconds
1978: 8086 Processor

The 8086 Microprocessor

In 1978, Intel released the 8086 microprocessor, a 16-bit CPU that would establish the x86 architecture‚Äîthe foundation for decades of processor development to follow.

When IBM selected the 8088 (a variant of the 8086) for its first personal computer in 1981, it set the stage for Intel's dominance in the PC market and established the x86 instruction set as the industry standard.

Key Features:
  • 16-bit processor
  • Clock speeds: 4.77 MHz to 10 MHz
  • 29,000 transistors using 3Œºm technology
  • 1MB addressable memory
  • Established the foundation for modern computing
1985: 386 Processor

The 386 Processor

In 1985, Intel introduced the 386 processor (officially the 80386), the first 32-bit x86 microprocessor. This groundbreaking chip revolutionized personal computing by enabling multitasking operating systems and addressing significantly more memory than its predecessors.

The 386 was a critical advancement that enabled the development of more sophisticated software, including Microsoft Windows, which required 32-bit processing capabilities.

Key Features:
  • 32-bit architecture
  • 275,000 transistors
  • Could address up to 4GB of memory
  • Clock speeds from 16MHz to 33MHz
  • Built-in memory management and protected mode
2008: Sustainability Summit

Intel's First Sustainability Summit

In 2008, Intel hosted its first Sustainability Summit, bringing together industry leaders, policymakers, and environmental experts to address the growing challenges of climate change and environmental sustainability in the technology sector.

This marked a significant shift in Intel's corporate focus, putting sustainability at the forefront of its business strategy and innovation efforts.

Key Outcomes:
  • Establishment of Intel's first comprehensive environmental goals
  • Commitment to reduce water usage and greenhouse gas emissions
  • Industry collaboration on sustainable practices
  • Development of more energy-efficient processors
  • Launch of several initiatives to improve supply chain sustainability
2012: Renewable Electricity Initiative

Leading in Renewable Energy

In 2012, Intel became the largest voluntary corporate purchaser of green power in the United States according to the U.S. EPA. The company committed to sourcing 100% of its electricity from renewable sources across its U.S. and European operations.

This milestone represented a major investment in wind, solar, hydro, and biomass electricity generation, significantly reducing Intel's carbon footprint.

Key Achievements:
  • Purchased more than 3.1 billion kWh of green power annually
  • Installed solar panels at multiple Intel campuses
  • Reduced carbon emissions by approximately 2.6 million metric tons
  • Received EPA Green Power Partner of the Year award
  • Invested in over 70 on-site renewable energy projects
2019: RISE Strategy

RISE: A New Corporate Strategy

In 2019, Intel launched its RISE strategy (Responsible, Inclusive, Sustainable, Enabling), establishing a comprehensive framework for corporate responsibility and environmental stewardship through 2030.

This strategic initiative represents Intel's holistic approach to creating a more sustainable and inclusive technology industry while addressing global environmental challenges.

Key Components:
  • Responsible Business: Ethical conduct, supply chain management, and human rights
  • Inclusive Workplace: Promoting diversity and inclusion within Intel and the tech industry
  • Sustainable Operations: Minimizing environmental impact in manufacturing and operations
  • Enabling Technology: Creating technology solutions that address global sustainability challenges

The RISE strategy set ambitious 2030 goals, including net positive water use, 100% renewable electricity, and zero waste to landfills across Intel's global operations.

2022: Net Zero Commitment

Net Zero Greenhouse Gas Emissions

In 2022, Intel announced its commitment to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions across its global operations by 2040. This ambitious pledge builds on the company's 2030 RISE goals and represents a significant escalation in Intel's climate action strategy.

The net zero roadmap includes interim milestones and concrete actions to dramatically reduce Intel's direct and indirect carbon emissions.

Key Commitments:
  • Achieve 100% renewable electricity use across global operations
  • Invest $300 million in energy conservation projects
  • Build new factories and facilities to meet U.S. Green Building Council LEED program standards
  • Work with suppliers to identify greenhouse gas reduction opportunities
  • Develop more sustainable and energy-efficient products
2023: GHG Emissions Reduction

Significant Progress in Emissions Reduction

In 2023, Intel reported a substantial 39% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions compared to 2019 levels. This achievement marks significant progress toward Intel's 2030 environmental goals and its larger commitment to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2040.

The reduction was accomplished through a combination of energy efficiency measures, renewable energy investments, and innovative approaches to manufacturing processes.

Key Achievements:
  • Implementation of advanced abatement systems for manufacturing emissions
  • Expansion of on-site renewable energy generation
  • Energy-efficient building upgrades across Intel campuses
  • New power purchase agreements for renewable electricity
  • Development of more energy-efficient chip manufacturing technologies
